TORRE DE
CONTROLE

Logistics operators were drowning in alerts. Critical delivery failures were buried under routine noise, causing cascading delays. We built an exception-first command center that tells them exactly what's wrong and how to fix it.

By replacing fragmented dashboards with a unified operational model, we shifted the team from reactive firefighting to proactive resolution.

Role Product Designer
Duration 6 Months
Context Logistics / B2B SaaS
Focus Incident Resolution & Ops
01 //

The Chaos

What made this hard

Logistics operators were flying blind. They relied on multiple disconnected tools to monitor deliveries, manage exceptions, and coordinate resolution. Critical alerts were buried under routine noise, and decisions that should take seconds were taking minutes — causing cascading downstream delays.

Signal Overload

Too many alerts, no hierarchy — operators couldn't distinguish critical from routine.

Fragmented Tools

Monitoring, dispatch, and resolution lived in different systems with no unified state.

High Human Error

Manual coordination under pressure led to missed exceptions and delayed responses.

Artifact: System Map / Operator Workflow

Show a diagram of the fragmented tools operators were using, or a workflow map highlighting the bottlenecks and manual steps. This proves you understand the complexity of the problem space.

↗ Evidence: Complexity → Clarity

Reframed fragmented monitoring signals into one operational model, helping teams read status quickly and choose the next action with less coordination overhead.

02 //

The System

Designing for exceptions

We threw away the traditional dashboard. Instead of showing everything, we designed an interface around what's wrong. Operators see only what needs action — everything else is suppressed by default.

Artifact: Exception-First UI / Wireframe

Show a wireframe or high-fidelity screen of the new interface, specifically highlighting how routine noise is suppressed and critical alerts are surfaced. This proves your ability to design for focus.

Exception-First Model

Designed the interface around what's wrong, not what's normal. Operators see only what needs action — everything else is suppressed by default.

Configurable Workflows

Each ops team could customize alert thresholds, escalation paths, and automation rules — preventing one-size-fits-all failures.

Contextual Alerts

Alerts carry context: what happened, what's at risk, suggested next action. Reduced decision time from minutes to seconds.

Automation Cues

Surface opportunities for automation: "This exception was resolved the same way 60 times — automate it?" — turning patterns into rules.

"Speed only matters when operators know what action to take. The system gives the context — humans make the call."

Design Principle

↗ Evidence: Trade-offs

Prioritized exception-first workflows over broad dashboard polish in V1 — reducing noise and ensuring the ops team could act fast on critical incidents.

03 //

The Resistance

Building trust in automation

Building a unified control center wasn't just a technical challenge; it was a cultural one. Operators were deeply skeptical of automation. They feared losing control.

Operator Trust Gap

Operators were deeply skeptical of automation. Every automated action needed a visible audit trail and one-click override to maintain trust.

Cross-Org Alignment

Dispatch, monitoring, and management each had contradictory success definitions. Required reframing KPIs around shared operational outcomes.

Real-Time Constraints

Interface decisions had to account for sub-second data refresh cycles without causing visual noise or distraction — a unique interaction design challenge.

Artifact: Audit Trail UI / Trust-Building Feature

Show a specific UI component or interaction flow that was designed specifically to build trust with operators, such as an audit log or a one-click override button. This proves your empathy for the user's fears.

↗ Evidence: Systems Thinking

Aligned exception handling, dispatch priorities, and monitoring signals in one operational model, reducing reaction time and improving predictability under peak load.

The Control

Measurable outcomes

By giving operators context instead of just data, we drastically reduced reaction times. The system learned from their decisions, eventually automating 60% of routine exceptions.
